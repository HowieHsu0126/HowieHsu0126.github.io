Recipe for a General, Powerful, Scalable Graph Transformer
2023-07-24|文献阅读
字数总计: 2k|阅读时长: 11 分钟

1 📑Metadata

摘要

We propose a recipe on how to build a general, powerful, scalable (GPS) graph Transformer with linear complexity and state-of-the-art results on a diverse set of benchmarks. Graph Transformers (GTs) have gained popularity in the ﬁeld of graph representation learning with a variety of recent publications but they lack a common foundation about what constitutes a good positional or structural encoding, and what differentiates them. In this paper, we summarize the different types of encodings with a clearer deﬁnition and categorize them as being local, global or relative. The prior GTs are constrained to small graphs with a few hundred nodes, here we propose the ﬁrst architecture with a complexity linear in the number of nodes and edges by decoupling the local real-edge aggregation from the fully-connected Transformer. We argue that this decoupling does not negatively affect the expressivity, with our architecture being a universal function approximator on graphs. Our GPS recipe consists of choosing 3 main ingredients: (i) positional/structural encoding, (ii) local message-passing mechanism, and (iii) global attention mechanism. We provide a modular framework GRAPHGPS1 that supports multiple types of encodings and that provides efﬁciency and scalability both in small and large graphs. We test our architecture on 16 benchmarks and show highly competitive results in all of them, show-casing the empirical beneﬁts gained by the modularity and the combination of different strategies.

2.4 有哪些相关研究？如何归类？谁是这一课题在领域内值得关注的研究员？

2.4.1 Graph Transformers (GT)

  1. Expected to help alleviate the problems of over-smoothing and over-squashing in MPNNs.
  2. Fully-connected Graph Transformer
    • Use eigenvectors of the graph Laplacian as the node positional encoding (PE).
  3. SAN
    1. Implement an invariant aggregation of Laplacian's eigenvectors for the PE.
    2. Use conditional attention for real and virtual edges of a graph.
  4. Graphormer
    • Use pair-wise graph distances (or 3D distances) to define relative positional encodings.
  5. GraphiT
    • Use relative PE derived from diffusion kernels to modulate the attention between nodes.
  6. GraphTrans: Hybrid architecture
    1. First use a stack of MPNN layers, before fully-connecting the graph.
    2. SAT, EGT, GRPE

2.4.2 Positional and Structural Encodings

  1. Previous works
    1. Laplacian PE
    2. Shortest-path-distance
    3. Node degree centrality
    4. Kernel distance
    5. Random-walk SE
    6. Structural-aware
  2. Categorization
  3. PE/SE are:
    1. used as [[soft bias]], meaning they are simply provided as input features.
    2. used to direct the messages or create bridges between distant nodes.

2.4.3 Linear Transformers

  1. Quadratic complexity of attention in the original Transformer architecture.
  2. Language modeling:
    1. Linformer
    2. Reformer
    3. Longformer
    4. Performer
    5. BigBird
  3. Graph domain:
    1. Performer-style attention kernelization

2.5.1 Modular Positional and Structural Encodings

PE and SE are different yet complementary. 1. PE gives a notion of distance. 2. SE gives a notion of structural similarity. 3. Global --> a text 4. Local --> a sentence 5. Relative --> distance between two words 6. Node --> a word

2.5.1.1 What is PE and SE?

  1. Positional Encodings (PE)
    1. Providing an idea of the position in space of a given node within the graph.
    2. Two close nodes within a graph or subgraph have close PE.
    3. Previous approach: Computing pair-wise distance between each pairs of nodes or their eigenvectors.
    4. Linear complexity PE should either be features of nodes or real edges of the graph.
      1. The eigenvectors of the graph Laplacian.
      2. Or their gradient.
  2. Structural Encodings (SE)
    1. Providing an embedding of the structure of graphs or subgraphs to help increase the expressivity and the generalizability of GNNs.
    2. Two nodes sharing similar subgraphs or two similar graphs have close SE.
    3. Previous approach:
      1. Identifying pre-defined patterns in the graphs as one-hot encodings Requiring expert knowledge of graphs.
      2. Using diagonal of the m-steps random-walk matrix.
      3. Using the eigenvalues of the Laplacian.

2.5.1.2 Local

Used as node features.

  1. Local PE:
    • Allow a node to know its position and role within a local cluster of nodes.
    • Within a cluster, the closer two nodes are to each other, the closer their local PE will be.
  2. Local SE:
    • Allow a node to understand what sub-structures it is a part of.
    • The more similar the m-hop subgraphs around two nodes are, the closer their local SE will be.

2.5.1.3 Global

Used as node or graph features.

  1. Global PE:
    • Allow a node to know its global position within a graph.
    • Within a graph, the closer two nodes are, the closer their global PE will be.
  2. Global SE:
    • Provide the network with information about the global structure of the graph.
    • The more similar two graphs are, the closer their global SE will be.

2.5.1.4 Relative

Used as edge features.

  1. Relative PE
    • Allow two nodes to understand their distances or directional relationships.
    • Edge embedding that is correlated to the distance given by any global or local PE.
  2. Relative SE
    • Allow two nodes to understand how much their structure differ.
    • Edge embedding that is correlated to the difference between any local SE.

2.5.2 Why Do We Need PE and SE in MPNN?

  1. Equivalence between 1-WL test and MPNNs --> limitations of expressive power.
  2. Using PE and SE as soft biases.

2.5.2.1 1-Weisfeiler-Leman test (1-WL)

[!Note] About 1-Weisfeiler-Leman test (1-WL) 1. Iteratively updating the color of a node based on its 1-hop local neighborhood 2. Until an iteration when the node colors do not change change successively. 3. The final histogram of the node colors determine whether the algorithm outputs the two graphs to be ‘non-isomorphic’.

[!Note] About Expressive power of MPNNs. 1. The aggregation and update function in MPNNs are at most powerful as the hash function of the [[1-WL test]]. 2. GIN: Graph Isomorphism Network.

  1. Standard MPNNs are as expressive as the [[1-WL test]]，failing to distinguish non-isomorphic graphs under a 1-hop aggregation
  2. Local, global and relative PE/SE allow MPNNs to become more expressive than the 1-WL test.

2.5.2.2 Circular Skip Link (CSL) graph

  1. 1-WL cannot distinguish the two non-isomorphic graphs. --> Neither does MPNN.
    1. Using a global PE assigns each node a unique initial color and makes the CSL graph-pair distinguishable.
    2. Using a local SE can successfully capture the difference in the skip links of the two graphs, resulting in their different node coloring.

2.5.2.3 The Decalin Molecular Graph

  1. 1-WL: The combination of the node colors of the node-sets will produce the same embedding for the two links.

  2. Using local PE is noneffective.

  3. Using a distance-based relative PE on the edges or an eigenvector-based global PE would successfully differentiate the embeddings of the two links.

Proposition: there exists Positional Encodings (PE) and Structural Encoding (SE) which MPNNs are not guaranteed to learn.

2.5.4 Theoretical Expressivity

2.5.4.1 Preserving edge information in the Transformer layer

  1. Previous GTs:
    1. Graph Transformer, SAN and Graphormer: only use edge features to condition the attention between node pairs.
    2. GraphiT: does not consider edge features at all.
    3. 2-step methods (GraphTrans and SAT): use edge features in the first MPNN step --> [[over-smoothing]]
  2. GPS:
    1. One round of local neighborhood aggregation via an MPNN layer.
    2. Global self-attention mechanism.
      1. Explicitly depends on node features.
      2. Implicitly encodes edge information.
    3. Reduces the initial representation bottleneck.
    4. Enables iterative local and global interactions.

2.5.4.2 Universal function approximator on graphs

  1. Given the full set of Laplacian eigenvectors, GTs was a universal function approximator on graphs and could provide an approximate solution to the isomorphism problem.
  2. More powerful than any WL isomorphism test given enough parameters.
